Torture Test

Failure 1 - Failure to adhere to build plate.

Failure 2 - Scrap filament disrupted build.

Failure 3 - Overly delicate points broke mid-build.

Failure 4 - Additional support points needed for increased scale.

Fifth attempt - Success!

My anticipated points of failure were the belly and nose overhangs, however, in practice I found that the first point of failure was that the hooves were too small to adhere to the plate, causing a machine-damaging backup. After adding a platform to the design for the second attempt, a loose piece of leftover material from a previous build on the printer used was being dragged by the nozzle, disrupting the build. For test 3, between platform and paint-on supports, my new points of failure centered on the delicacy of many parts of the print, such as the ear and the connection point between the tail and the body - a concern that could be mitigated by scaling up the design. In attempt 4, one hoof did not print properly, this could have been human error as I believed its support structure was improperly adhered in the early stages and removed it to avoid a blockage. The tail also fell out of place at some point near the end and was not adhered properly to the rest of the structure. In attempt five the print is scaled up again for structural integrity, and supports are added under the hooves, a measure apparently necessitated by the added height of the body supports. Build has gone from thirty minutes and 4 grams of filament in attempt one to three hours and 40 grams of filament in attempt five.

Splicing and Fusion

TinkerCAD render comprised of rotated rings and inverted cone.

Printed basket structure.

This splicing project made entirely in TinkerCAD is comprised of rings rotated around their center point and a conical hole inverted to form a basket style structure, both basic shapes available in TinkerCAD. During printing, supports were used on the lower half of the print but not the upper half, causing some sagging in the first unsupported horizontal layers, but no significant failure of the print.

The Fusion/TinkerCAD hybrid project is comprised of a basic carabiner and a heavily modified hoof pick layered with hollow cylinders to shape the handle around the carabiner and the edges rounded out. Print is purely aesthetic, as the thin plastic of the working edge of the pick could not withstand real use and the carabiner was not printer with functional joints. A more usable version would be steel with a simpler pick hollowed in the center to avoid extra weight.

Alarm Clock

Alarm clock was soldered from a pre-made kit. Measurements were taken with digital calipers for overall structure and for display window, buttons, charger port, and speaker. 3D Printed box was made from scratch in Fusion primarily using the sketch and extrusion functions, and an open-source model of a rooster was added to the lid. Print went smoothly, but when removing the supports from the rooster the legs broke, leaving the box with just chicken feet on top. In intital print the box was slightly too short, so I returned to the original model to add height and kept the first lid print. Following reprint, all components fit together well, and the clock and lid slide into place with just enough effort to keep them in place unless they are being deliberately removed. Two attempted laser cuts failed, due to the fact that the structure was made as a whole before being separated and laid flat in fusion, failures were likely due to the construction lines. Pieces can not be assembled in any recognizable capacity.

Prosthetic Hand

The printing of the prosthetic hand components went smoothly, with only minor drooping in the first layers overhanging the internal section of the palm. Construction was significantly more difficult. I chose to assemble the hand without referencing guides beforehand as a puzzle for myself, which was fairly simple in itself, however, the non-printed components were not all correctly sized for the print. It is possible the scaling-up process slightly overshot the mark where the more precise measurements came into play. I was unable to find screws wide enough to hold the tensioner rods in place under any pressure in the maker spaces on campus such as the library or art building, so in order to make the hand functional the looser-fitting components were attached with superglue. The hand, while uncomfortable and relatively weak whether the user has fingers or not, does now function and while it cannot be tuned by someone who does not have well-coordinated fingers, it can be used to manipulate objects with roughly the strength and finesse of a small child.

The weakness of the grip provided by the eNable hand stood out to me, particularlly because it took so much effort to achieve the very low maximum possible. This model prosthetic obviously lacks the optimizations present in an organic human hand, and a simple improvement to at least decrease the effort necessary to achieve the maximum grip strength would be to add a pulley effect over where the knuckles would be or at the wrist joint. This could be a rod capable of spinning between two points or simply an elevated ridge, either would improve ease of use, especially since strength through the user's wrist should not be assumed. This change would place day to day tasks such as manipulating stiff door handles or lifting water bottles within range of more users, even if it would not make a wider range of tasks available to users already capable of achieving the hand's maximum grip strength. Reducing the force necessary to operate the hand would also generally assist with coordination, which often prove a steep learning curve to users learning how to manipulate extremities with no sensation. Another area for further improvement would be the creation of specialized attachments or entire hands fine-tuned for specific hobbies, jobs, or domestic tasks. Of course, strength could likely be more easily achieved in another material, but the cost accessibility of printed hands, particularly for growing children, is a benefit that cannot be understated.

Campus Accessibility

Historical Specificity - The chapels had great thought put into their architectural philosophies in regards to the way they never cast shadows on each other, their identical dimensions, etc.. Simultaneously, they are a relic of their time in that the grand gesture of inclusivity is solely judeochristian.

Environmental Sustainibility - A bike shelter and repair station and bus stops which doubles for mobility aids for both age and ability, both in regards to access to transport and for the sheltered area with a bench. Both get extra points for economic accessibility, a common theme in environmentally sustainable infrastructure which is made available to the public.

Wheeled Mobility Device - Access points also benefit any lower or whole body mobility limits whether related to age, chronic conditions, or injury as people transporting large objects. (such as geese :))

Limtited Vision - Road crossing safety is improved with a tactile navigation aid and sound indicator of safe(er) crossing times.

Cultural Inclusivity - Cultural inclusivity is predictably varied, with accommodation of dietary restrictions, space for religous observance, and public celebration of diversity. Dining which accommodates strict religious dietary restrictions can also provide a safe food source for those with certain allergies, regardless of religious observance.

Children - While college campuses have little built infrastructure specifically intended for children, it's unsurprising that many would bring small objects with the sorts of colors and patterns primarily intended to appeal to young children as a source of comfort in a new environment or to liven a cinderblock dorm. A selection of children's books in the library may also provide a valuable source of entertainment for visiting children.

Health and Wellness - Health and wellness encompasses everything from emergency safety measures such as fire extinguishers and sprinklers and medical devices like AEDs, to basic sanitation measures like bathrooms and garbage cans, to smoke-free zones.

The allergy pantry is simultaneously a good and bad example of accessibility- on one hand it's a safe location for the most extreme allergies, on the other hand, information on getting access to the pantry can be hard to find.

Exemplary Inaccessibility - Dorms built to be riot proof are shockingly (/s) not the most accessible, here we have two examples assuming a lack of assistance, including stairs with no alternative access point to the upper floors present in the entire building, and a hallway so narrow it is inconvenient for two able-bodied people to pass each other, which has no room for assistive technology like a wheelchair or for human aid, and would make it extremely difficult to remove an incapacitated individual from the building in a medical emergency.

Mobile Phone Addiction

Interviews - both of my initial interviews were with people who used their phones responsibly and felt no issues with their personal use of mobible devices, providing little insight into the problem of addiction but good insight into what healthy use can look like.

Based on the interviews conducted by my team, we concluded that in order for our product to be effective, the user must fulfill the following criteria: A: Aware that they use their phone more than they should, B: Motivated to reduce their phone use, and C: Has the ability to leave their phone inaccessible to normal use for somewhat predictable periods of time.

The basic structure of the design is simple, a hinged box with a timer-controlled lock to block access for whatever amount of time is entered, as well as openings for a charger port, for accessing the power button to call emergency services, and, in later design iterations, access points along the front to allow for accepting or declining incoming calls. and for viewing the caller ID and using the phone speaker. The box structure is straightforward to print from a fusion file according to the parameters of the particular model of phone, or to mold in a commercial manufacture setting. The timer lock would need to be added afterwards.

Build Guide

Product validation process could not be completed due to not having a working model. Process for product validation with a working model would be to identify peers fitting the criteria determined after the interview process, providing them with a functional model and an explanation of its operation, and comparing before an after screentime averages as well as conducting qualitative analysis through interviews. The primary source of criticism in initial interviews was that the system requires user discipline in order to work, and while this is true of our system as well as most other systems that do not require a second person as an administrator, the physical design is intended to both reverse the impulsivity factor, turning making phone use inaccessible into something that can be done on impulse, rather than the typical issue of phone use being the impulse decision, and to avoid the workarounds available in self-regulated software systems. Therefore, the primary identifying trait of a potential candidate for testing or use is someone seeking out ways to reduce their phone use already, particularly if they have not found systems with overrides available to be effective.
