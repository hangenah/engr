Torture Test

Failure 1 - Failure to adhere to build plate.

Failure 2 - Scrap filament disrupted build.

Failure 3 - Overly delicate points broke mid-build.

Failure 4 - Additional support points needed for increased scale.

Fifth attempt - Success!

My anticipated points of failure were the belly and nose overhangs, however, in practice I found that the first point of failure was that the hooves were too small to adhere to the plate, causing a machine-damaging backup. After adding a platform to the design for the second attempt, a loose piece of leftover material from a previous build on the printer used was being dragged by the nozzle, disrupting the build. For test 3, between platform and paint-on supports, my new points of failure centered on the delicacy of many parts of the print, such as the ear and the connection point between the tail and the body - a concern that could be mitigated by scaling up the design. In attempt 4, one hoof did not print properly, this could have been human error as I believed its support structure was improperly adhered in the early stages and removed it to avoid a blockage. The tail also fell out of place at some point near the end and was not adhered properly to the rest of the structure. In attempt five the print is scaled up again for structural integrity, and supports are added under the hooves, a measure apparently necessitated by the added height of the body supports. Build has gone from thirty minutes and 4 grams of filament in attempt one to three hours and 40 grams of filament in attempt five.

Splicing and Fusion

TinkerCAD render comprised of rotated rings and inverted cone.

Printed basket structure.

This splicing project made entirely in TinkerCAD is comprised of rings rotated around their center point and a conical hole inverted to form a basket style structure, both basic shapes available in TinkerCAD. During printing, supports were used on the lower half of the print but not the upper half, causing some sagging in the first unsupported horizontal layers, but no significant failure of the print.

The Fusion/TinkerCAD hybrid project is comprised of a basic carabiner and a heavily modified hoof pick layered with hollow cylinders to shape the handle around the carabiner and the edges rounded out. Print is purely aesthetic, as the thin plastic of the working edge of the pick could not withstand real use and the carabiner was not printer with functional joints. A more usable version would be steel with a simpler pick hollowed in the center to avoid extra weight.

Alarm Clock

Alarm clock was soldered from a pre-made kit. Measurements were taken with digital calipers for overall structure and for display window, buttons, charger port, and speaker. 3D Printed box was made from scratch in Fusion primarily using the sketch and extrusion functions, and an open-source model of a rooster was added to the lid. Print went smoothly, but when removing the supports from the rooster the legs broke, leaving the box with just chicken feet on top. In intital print the box was slightly too short, so I returned to the original model to add height and kept the first lid print. Following reprint, all components fit together well, and the clock and lid slide into place with just enough effort to keep them in place unless they are being deliberately removed. ADD LASER CUT.

Campus Accessibility

historical specificity - chapels had great thought put in with the whole not casting shadows, identical dimensions, etc., while simultaneously being a relic of their time in that the grand symbol of inclusivity is solely judeochristian

environmental sustainibility w/ bike shelter and repair station, bus stop doubles for mobility (age and ability, both for access to transport and for sheltered area with bench) and both get extra points for economic accessibility

wheeled mobility device access, also benefits any lower or whole body mobility limits age/chronic/injury and people transporting large objects

safer crosswalk for limited vision, with a tactile navigation aid and sound indicator of safe(r) crossing times

design for cultural inclusivity - accommodation of dietary restrictions, space for religous observance, public celebration of diversity. strict religious dietary restrictions can also provide a safe food source for those with certain allergies

while college campuses have little built infrastructure specifically intended for children, it's unsurprising that many would bring small objects with the sorts of colors and patterns primarily intended to appeal to young children as a source of comfort in a new environment or to liven a cinderblock dorm.

health and wellness encompasses everything from safety measures such as fire extinguishers to medical devices like AEDs to basic sanitation measures like bathrooms and garbage cans

the allergy pantry is simultaneously a good and bad example of accessibility- on one hand it's a safe location for the most extreme allergies, on the other hand, information on getting access to the pantry can be hard to find.

dorms built to be riot proof are shockingly not the most accessible, here we have two examples assuming a lack of assistance, including stairs with no alternative in the entire building, and a hallway in which it is inconvenient for two able-bodied people to pass each other, there is no room for assistive technology like a wheelchair or for human aid
